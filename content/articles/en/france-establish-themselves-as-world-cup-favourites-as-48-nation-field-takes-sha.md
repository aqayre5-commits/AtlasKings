France have positioned themselves as the tournament favourites in the opening months of World Cup qualification, according to a comprehensive global assessment of all 48 nations set to compete in 2026. The ranking, compiled by football writers and analysts from across the world, reflects not just France's pedigree as defending champions but their clear depth of attacking talent—a point underscored this week when Kylian Mbappé warned that the tournament will feature "more talent and potential than in 2022." That assessment carries weight when delivered by a player of Mbappé's calibre, and it comes backed by France's recent performances: a 2-1 victory over Brazil despite playing an hour with ten men, followed by a comfortable 3-1 win over Colombia in which manager Didier Deschamps rotated his entire starting XI without sacrificing attacking threat.

The scale of France's attacking arsenal is almost overwhelming. Against Colombia, Deschamps fielded Marcus Thuram, Désiré Doué, Rayan Cherki, and Maghnes Akliouche in advanced positions, with Doué scoring twice in a straightforward victory. The manager himself acknowledged the embarrassment of riches at his disposal, stating that he was "well aware that there are some very good players that I won't be bringing because, in my opinion, there are even better ones." This is the statement of a coach working with a generation of players who, at the club level, are operating at the highest European standard. The 2026 World Cup will expand to 48 teams, diluting the overall quality of competition, but France's depth suggests they will navigate that landscape with considerable comfort.

Beyond France's dominance, the power rankings reveal a tournament field in flux. Senegal and Japan have secured positions in the top ten, signalling the geographical breadth of competitive football at the international level. Africa continues to produce world-class talent, and while Algeria's long rivalry with Morocco has defined North African football for decades, Morocco's own trajectory—including their run to the World Cup semi-finals in 2022—ensures they remain a serious contender in any expanded format. The Moroccan national team's continued development and the emergence of young players in European leagues means North Africa will have genuine claims to represent the continent's interests in 2026.

The expansion to 48 teams fundamentally reshapes tournament mathematics and creates more room for surprise packages and lesser-heeled nations to make their mark. Yet France's current position suggests that expanded field or not, the traditional football powers retain the structural advantages—depth of talent, coaching stability, and competitive infrastructure—that matter most when tournaments reach their knockout stages. Mbappé's warning about talent abundance appears aimed not at dismissing rivals but at acknowledging that France will face stronger opposition than in previous cycles. How that talent translates across a more diluted group stage and into knockout football remains the central question as 2026 approaches.
