The 2021 Africa Cup of Nations final between Morocco and Senegal, played in Casablanca in early February, should have been remembered as a showcase for African football. Instead, it has become a cautionary tale about how quickly institutional failures and national tensions can unravel the legitimacy of the continent's premier tournament. Senegal won on the field, claiming their first AFCON title with a penalty shootout victory after a 0–0 draw. But what followed—CAF investigations, disputed rulings, fan arrests, and now proceedings at the Court of Arbitration for Sport—has cast a shadow over that achievement and exposed fundamental problems in how the African game is governed.

The trouble began during the match itself. Moroccan supporters, denied entry or turned away from a stadium that was ostensibly their home ground, created scenes of chaos outside the Stade Ahmadou Ahidjo in Casablanca. Inside, there were credible reports of security lapses, crowd disorder, and inadequate stewarding. When Senegal prevailed in the penalty shootout, the narrative shifted quickly. Rather than celebrating the Teranga Lions' accomplishment, commentary turned to questions about whether Morocco had been treated fairly as hosts, whether the security environment had been compromised, and whether the match could fairly be called a "home" final at all. For Moroccan fans and officials alike, the sense was less one of defeat than of being let down by the machinery supposed to protect the integrity of the tournament.

What distinguished this escalation from typical post-match complaints was the institutional response. CAF began an investigation into the conduct of the final, examining everything from security arrangements to crowd management to potential misconduct by either federation. These inquiries, rather than clarifying matters, became a further point of contention. Each CAF ruling seemed to generate counter-arguments, appeals, and accusations of bias. Moroccan officials pursued their grievances through available channels, including appeals processes within CAF itself. Meanwhile, fan arrests related to the match's aftermath added a layer of diplomatic tension, with both countries' governments becoming invested in the outcome. Calls for boycotts emerged in some quarters, and the dispute began to assume the character of a bilateral issue rather than a purely sporting one.

The escalation to the Court of Arbitration for Sport represents a watershed moment. That Morocco and Senegal have felt compelled to take their case to CAS suggests that trust in CAF's internal mechanisms has broken down sufficiently to warrant international legal arbitration. The court will have to weigh complex questions about match administration, security responsibility, and whether any procedural failures materially affected the result. For the wider African game, the implications are serious. If major tournaments routinely devolve into legal disputes that outlast the euphoria of victory, sponsors will hesitate, broadcasters will question their investment, and the prestige of AFCON itself will suffer.

For Morocco specifically, the case represents something deeper than one lost match. Hosting AFCON—a tournament that Morocco has not won since 1976—carried expectations of redemption. The security failures and subsequent institutional fractures have instead become part of a narrative of institutional dysfunction at home and abroad. Whether CAS ultimately rules in Morocco's favor or not, the reputational damage to both the kingdom's hosting capability and to CAF's credibility will take years to repair.

The outcome of the CAS proceedings will likely arrive in 2024. When it does, it will say something profound not just about this match, but about whether African football's governing bodies can be trusted to administer tournaments fairly. For now, a victory that should have belonged to Senegal remains contested territory.
