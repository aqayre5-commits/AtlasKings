FIFA has removed Jean-Jacques Ndala from its roster of referees for the 2026 World Cup in the United States, Canada, and Mexico. The decision comes roughly two months before the tournament begins, and follows a controversy surrounding Ndala's performance at the recent Africa Cup of Nations final. The removal represents a significant setback for the Congolese official, who had earned selection to one of football's biggest stages—a credential that typically marks the apex of a referee's career.

The specifics of what occurred at the AFCON final that triggered FIFA's action remain somewhat opaque from the available reporting, but the timing and decisiveness of the response signal that FIFA's referees' committee viewed the incident as serious enough to warrant immediate removal from the World Cup roster. AFCON finals are high-stakes matches watched across the continent, and the pressure on match officials is immense. Whatever decision or decisions Ndala made during that final match appears to have fallen short of FIFA's standards for the tournament's threshold.

Removing a referee two months before a World Cup is not routine. It speaks to either a significant individual mistake or a broader pattern of concern that the committee felt could not be overlooked at such a prominent event. World Cup matches are scrutinized by millions globally, with every contentious call amplified by media analysis and social debate. FIFA's referee selection process is deliberately rigorous; by the time officials are named to a World Cup squad, they have typically proven themselves across multiple competitive cycles and tournaments. To strip that credential away so close to the event underscores the seriousness with which FIFA treated the AFCON matter.

For African referees, selection to a World Cup represents validation on the world stage and a career-defining achievement. The pool of African officials regularly selected to major tournaments remains relatively small, and each removal reduces representation from the continent. Ndala's departure from the roster opens a spot that will likely be filled by another official—possibly another African referee, though this depends on FIFA's balancing of continental representation across its final squad. The decision also sends a message to match officials across Africa: even success at the continental level does not guarantee immunity from accountability if FIFA determines performance has fallen short.

The 2026 World Cup will be the first held across three nations and will expand to 48 teams, creating more matches and requiring a larger contingent of referees than previous tournaments. The expanded format means FIFA had already assembled an unusually large roster. Ndala's removal, while significant for him personally and professionally, does not leave the tournament critically understaffed. Nevertheless, the incident highlights the pressure officials face and FIFA's willingness to enforce its standards even at the last moment before such a major event.

As the tournament approaches, the focus will now shift to how the remaining referees prepare for what promises to be a complex, sprawling competition. For Ndala, the path forward will require demonstrating significant improvement and recovery of trust before he can expect selection to future major tournaments.
