The dispute centres on circumstances that led to the default award—a matter that has created an unusual and unresolved situation in African football's most prestigious competition.

The path to this ruling reveals the complexity of continental football governance. The 2025 Africa Cup of Nations final, the match that would determine the champion, apparently did not reach a conclusion through normal play. Rather than contest the outcome on the pitch, CAF's procedural mechanisms resulted in a default award to Morocco. This administrative decision, while technically sound under confederation rules, has left Senegal in the position of questioning whether a tournament won by forfeit carries the same legitimacy as one settled through competition. The Senegalese position reflects a broader tension in sports law: the distinction between what a governing body rules and what stakeholders believe reflects the true merit of competition.

From a regulatory standpoint, CAF's Appeals Committee decision carries formal authority within African football's structure. Default awards are not uncommon in continental competitions when teams fail to meet fixture requirements or breach competition regulations. However, Fal's public insistence that Senegal retains the title despite this ruling suggests the federation believes either the circumstances warranted an exception or that the default decision itself may be vulnerable to further legal challenge. Whether Senegal intends to pursue additional appeals through CAF's structures or explore arbitration remains unclear, but their resistance signals they do not view the matter as settled. Such disputes, when they persist at federation level, can create awkward situations where two nations claim the same trophy.

These tensions affect not only Morocco and Senegal but the credibility of the continent's flagship tournament.
