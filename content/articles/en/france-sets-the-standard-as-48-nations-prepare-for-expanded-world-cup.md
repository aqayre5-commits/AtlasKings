France has emerged as the frontrunner in early assessments of the 2026 World Cup field, a ranking that reflects not merely their current form but the extraordinary abundance of attacking talent at Didier Deschamps' disposal. The reigning European champions demonstrated this depth across two recent fixtures, beating Brazil 2-1 despite playing the entire second half with ten men, then dismantling Colombia 3-1 with an entirely reshuffled starting XI. Kylian Mbappé's observation that there is "more talent and potential than in 2022" carries particular weight—it suggests France's squad has evolved rather than merely maintained itself, a distinction that matters when examining which nations will contend for the trophy in North America.

The gulf between France's resources and those of most other nations is evident in Deschamps' casual ability to rotate. Against Colombia, he fielded an attack featuring Marcus Thuram, Désiré Doué, Rayan Cherki, and Maghnes Akliouche—a lineup that would constitute a first-choice front four for many World Cup participants. Doué's two goals underscored the clinical finishing available. Yet Deschamps himself has acknowledged the impossible arithmetic of squad selection: "I'm well aware that there are some very good players that I won't be bringing because, in my opinion, there are even better ones." This is the luxury problem of French football at present. It also means that depth, not just starting quality, will be a decisive factor in Qatar's successor tournament.

The inclusion of Senegal and Japan in the top ten of these preliminary rankings indicates how the 48-team format has broadened the competitive base. Senegal's ability to reach the Africa Cup of Nations final in January demonstrated consistent quality across the continent, while Japan has maintained technical sophistication and organizational discipline that punches above typical expectations for Asian sides. Yet France's position atop the hierarchy remains unshaken—the combination of individual talent, tactical flexibility, and the burden of defending their status creates a clear favorites' bracket.

For Morocco, the expanded format presents both opportunity and complexity. The Atlas Lions qualified comfortably for 2026 and arrive as Africa's highest-ranked nation on most metrics, having reached the World Cup semi-finals in 2022 and the Africa Cup of Nations final earlier this year. Against a France that continues to raise its own ceiling, Morocco would need to maintain the defensive discipline and transitional speed that have defined their recent tournament performances. The regional rivalry with Algeria adds another layer—while Algeria secured one of Africa's five spots, Morocco's trajectory suggests they are better positioned to challenge in the later stages. Early power rankings always shift as tournaments approach, but they serve a useful function in framing the conversations that will define the next eighteen months of qualification and preparation.

As the international calendar fills with friendlies and competitive fixtures across all confederations, these assessments will be tested constantly. France's depth is real, but sustainability across a longer tournament and potential injury management remain variables. Senegal and Japan will need to prove their rankings are more than early-window form. For Morocco, the challenge is familiar: compete at the level they showed in 2022, execute efficiently in knockout football, and prove that African football can sustain a genuine challenge to Europe's established powers.
