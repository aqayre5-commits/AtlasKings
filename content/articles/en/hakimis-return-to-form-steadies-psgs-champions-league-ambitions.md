Achraf Hakimi is finding his rhythm again. The Moroccan international, operating as PSG's right-back in Luis Enrique's system, has rediscovered the aggressive attacking form that made him one of Europe's most dangerous full-backs, according to reporting from L'Équipe on Thursday. With Paris Saint-Germain now entering the business end of the Champions League, Hakimi's resurgence comes at precisely the moment when the French club needs its best players firing. The recent performance against Liverpool FC underscored how integral he has become to PSG's attacking architecture—not merely a defender who occasionally ventures forward, but a genuine creative and offensive threat operating in the wide spaces.

Hakimi's career trajectory to this point has never been linear. After establishing himself at Real Madrid's academy and then flourishing at Borussia Dortmund, he joined Inter Milan where he won the Serie A title as part of an exceptional defensive unit. His move to PSG in 2021 was meant to be the next step into genuine European elite company, yet injuries, form fluctuations, and tactical adjustments under successive managers complicated that narrative. The player has always possessed the technical gifts—his dribbling, his intelligence in positioning, his ability to both create and finish chances—but consistency at the highest level requires more than talent. It requires fitness, confidence, and a tactical setup that maximizes your strengths. Under Enrique's watch, Hakimi appears to have found that balance again.

What makes his current form particularly significant is the role he plays within PSG's broader attacking structure. Full-backs in modern football are as much creators as defenders, and in a club stacked with attacking talent—Mbappé, Neymar's legacy lingers in the system, Barcola—the full-backs often determine whether PSG can break down deep defenses or create overload situations. Against Liverpool, Hakimi and his fellow full-back Nuno Mendes operated as "key engines" of attacking play. This terminology matters. It suggests Hakimi isn't simply participating in PSG's dominance; he is generating it. The performance suggests he has regained the burst of pace, the decisiveness in the final third, and the consistency in his positioning that characterized his best periods at Inter.

For Morocco's national team, Hakimi's resurgence at club level carries obvious importance. The Atlas Lions will face qualifiers and tournaments in the coming seasons, and their fullback options—both defensive and creative dimensions—require players performing at the highest levels of club football. A Hakimi playing with confidence at PSG is a Hakimi available for Morocco in peak condition.

As PSG progresses deeper into the Champions League, Hakimi's form becomes part of the question that defines European football in spring: can the French champions finally deliver the competition's trophy. Hakimi alone cannot answer that question, but a full-back combining defensive solidity with genuine attacking potency certainly helps. The next knockout stages will reveal whether his current form is a temporary uptick or a genuine restoration of the player PSG signed three years ago.
