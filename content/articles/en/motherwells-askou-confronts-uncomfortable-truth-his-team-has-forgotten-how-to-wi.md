Jens Berthel Askou is not in the business of mathematical projections. When asked whether Motherwell might accumulate enough points to secure a fourth-place finish in the Scottish Premiership, the manager declined to play the numbers game, preferring instead to address a more fundamental problem: his team has stopped winning matches. It is a candid admission from a coach who has inherited a club in transition and finds himself wrestling with form that oscillates between the acceptable and the deeply frustrating.

The admission cuts to the heart of Motherwell's season. Fourth place would represent a respectable finish by historical standards, a Champions League qualification spot that few outside the Old Firm and Celtic Park could reasonably claim. But Askou's refusal to set a target suggests he understands that chasing a points total is a backward-looking exercise. What matters now is recalibrating the present: restoring the psychological confidence that comes from consecutive wins, the kind of momentum that transforms a middle-table side into genuine contenders. When a manager says his team "misses winning," he is diagnosing a condition that no single tactical adjustment can cure.

Motherwell's season has been marked by the kind of inconsistency that defines mid-table Scottish football. They have shown flashes of quality sufficient to beat anyone on their day, yet they have also suffered defeats to sides they ought to dominate. This volatility is exhausting for supporters and, clearly, for the manager tasked with explaining it. Askou's candour suggests frustration with his players' ability to sustain performance levels across ninety minutes and across multiple fixtures. Winning streaks are built on habit—the collective understanding that success breeds success, that another three points is within reach because the last three came so recently. Motherwell's current position, somewhere in the middle of the pack, reflects an inability to establish that rhythm.

The manager's refusal to speculate on points targets also reveals something about his philosophy. Rather than set arbitrary numbers and hope his players chase them, Askou seems intent on addressing the psychological and tactical foundations of performance. It is a more austere approach than some fans might prefer, but it is also the language of someone trying to build sustainable improvement rather than chase quick fixes. Motherwell's path forward will be determined not by whether they reach a particular points total, but by whether they can rediscover the winning mentality that—on Askou's own admission—has temporarily deserted them.

The challenge now is whether Motherwell can reverse this trend before the season's defining moments arrive. Their remaining fixtures will tell us whether Askou's diagnosis is accurate and whether his side possesses the character to respond. Fourth place may yet be achievable, but only if they remember how to win.
