Scotland have confirmed their final pre-World Cup friendly, scheduling a match against Bolivia in Harrison, New Jersey on 6 June. The fixture will be broadcast live on BBC, giving the Scottish public a chance to see their squad in action one last time before heading to the tournament. It represents the closing piece of Scotland's preparation puzzle, a chance for Steve Clarke to fine-tune selection decisions and work through any remaining tactical adjustments with the squad in match conditions rather than the training ground.

The timing of this fixture sits perfectly within the typical World Cup preparation window. With the tournament beginning shortly after, Scotland needed a final opponent who could provide meaningful competition without the risk of injuries that might come against a top-tier side playing for tournament seeding. Bolivia, ranked 47th in the world, offers reasonable opposition while keeping the focus on rhythm and shape rather than defensive crisis management. Playing in the United States also makes logistical sense; it keeps Scotland's travel manageable and avoids the fatigue of flying back to Europe and then across the Atlantic again so close to the tournament.

This warm-up comes at a critical juncture for Clarke's planning. By early June, the manager will have a clearer picture of injury status across his squad, will have seen how key players have fared in their club seasons' run-ins, and will have narrowed down tactical approach based on group opponents and likely knockout scenarios. A match against Bolivia allows Scotland to test formations, experiment with combinations, and ensure match fitness is where it needs to be without the pressure of knockout football. The BBC coverage adds another layer of preparation value: players get accustomed to the intensity of a televised international, and Scotland's supporters get meaningful content in the countdown to the tournament proper.

For context, Scotland qualified for the World Cup through the UEFA playoff route, demonstrating the resilience and character that has become a hallmark of Clarke's tenure since 2019. Warm-up friendlies in this window are rarely about discovering new talent; they are about confirmation, consolidation, and confidence-building. Bolivia, while not among the world's elite, plays competitive football and won't be a walkover, which means Scotland must approach the match with professionalism rather than complacency.

Looking ahead, this Bolivia fixture marks the end of Scotland's preparation phase. Once the match concludes, focus shifts entirely to the World Cup itself. The weeks between 6 June and the tournament's opening matches will be used for final recovery, tactical refinement in training, and mental preparation. Clarke's selections from this period—both who plays and who gets extended minutes—will send clear signals about his thinking heading into the tournament. For Scotland's supporters and media, this friendly offers the last chance to assess the squad before the real business begins.
