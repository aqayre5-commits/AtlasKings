The 2021 Africa Cup of Nations final in Casablanca was supposed to be a celebration of Moroccan football. Instead, it has become the focal point of an unresolved institutional crisis that continues to ripple across African football. What began as a match Senegal won 4–2 on penalty kicks has since evolved into a complex dispute involving disciplinary rulings, international arbitration, fan violence, boycott threats, and fundamental questions about how the Confederation of African Football conducts its business. The case now sits before the Court of Arbitration for Sport, with no resolution in sight and no clear timeline for one.

The roots of the escalation lie not in the match itself—Senegal earned their victory—but in what happened before, during, and after it. Reports of security failures, organizational lapses, and crowd incidents at the Stade de Casablanca have become entangled with questions about whether the match was properly governed under CAF's own regulations. Morocco's subsequent appeals and challenges to CAF's handling of the final have widened the scope of the dispute far beyond the ninety minutes of football. What might have been a difficult loss to digest became a catalyst for broader scrutiny of how the continental body operates, how it enforces its rules, and whether those rules are applied consistently across member nations.

The timeline of escalation reveals a pattern familiar to anyone who has followed African football administration: initial decisions made under pressure, appeals filed, institutional contradictions exposed, and the involvement of external arbitration bodies that operate by different standards and timelines than the continental federation. Each step has generated new friction points. Fan arrests followed the match itself. Calls for boycotts emerged as positions hardened. CAF issued rulings; Morocco disputed them. Appeals went to CAS, where the case has lingered with the deliberative pace characteristic of international sports arbitration. The longer the dispute remains unresolved, the more it becomes not just about one match but about confidence in CAF's governance structures themselves.

For Moroccan football, the case represents a moment of institutional challenge. Morocco is a football power on the continent, with deep investment in its national team and a desire to host major tournaments. The AFCON final was supposed to be a showcase; instead, it became a symbol of how quickly things can unravel when organizational structures and dispute resolution mechanisms are perceived as inadequate. The Moroccan federation's willingness to pursue the case through CAS reflects not just disappointment over a loss but concerns about governance that extend beyond this single match. For Senegal, who won on the field, the lingering dispute casts an unnecessary shadow over their achievement.

The broader implication is uncomfortable for African football. CAF's reputation depends on being seen as competent, transparent, and fair. When a final—the competition's marquee event, watched across the continent—becomes the basis for an ongoing institutional dispute, it raises questions about the federation's capacity to manage high-stakes events. Whether CAS ultimately rules in Morocco's favor, Senegal's, or finds a middle ground may matter less than whether African football institutions can resolve such crises more decisively and with greater clarity in the future. Until that happens, the Morocco–Senegal case will remain a cautionary tale about what happens when governance structures break down under pressure.
