Brazil has not won a major tournament since 2007. That statistic haunts the nation's football culture. The Copa América is the most immediate opportunity for Dorival Júnior's regime to announce itself as a legitimate contender, not just a transitional team. Davide's presence in that backroom reflects a deliberate choice: bringing in someone who has worked alongside his father at Real Madrid and Everton, who understands how elite teams operate under pressure, and who can offer a fresh analytical perspective within a traditional structure. At 27, he is young enough to connect with the squad's emerging generation but experienced enough to have seen title-winning environments up close.

His father's influence is evident in the approach: Carlo Ancelotti has always believed that simplicity and structure create space for individual brilliance. That philosophy, when applied to a Brazilian squad rich in attacking talent, becomes a compelling framework. The assistant coach's role is to help translate that vision into training-ground reality and, crucially, into match execution under the noise and expectation of tournament football.

The broader context matters here. Brazil's recent campaigns have often felt tactically confused—caught between wanting to play attacking football and needing defensive solidity. The appointment of Dorival Júnior, and the infrastructure around him, represents an attempt to resolve that tension. Having someone like Davide in the room, comfortable working with data, video analysis, and the kind of meticulous preparation that defines modern football at the highest level, is not a romantic choice.
