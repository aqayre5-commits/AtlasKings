CAF President Patrice Motsepe completed a two-country tour of West Africa this week, visiting both Senegal and Morocco as the continent's football body grapples with fallout from the ongoing dispute over the 2025 Africa Cup of Nations final. The visits revealed markedly different messaging: in Dakar, Motsepe addressed corruption allegations directly; in Rabat, he pivoted to broader concerns about CAF's governance structure and its relationship with Morocco. The contrasting tenor of his engagements underscores how the AFCON dispute has metastasized beyond a single competition into a wider crisis of institutional credibility within African football.

The 2025 AFCON final controversy has accumulated political and legal dimensions that extend far beyond the pitch. What began as a scheduling or logistical question has snowballed into accusations of mismanagement and allegations of corruption within CAF itself. Motsepe's need to visit both nations separately—and with carefully calibrated messaging—reflects the sensitivity of the moment. Morocco's position in this dispute carries particular weight: the country has hosted continental tournaments before and possesses both the infrastructure and footballing infrastructure that CAF depends on. Any fracture in the relationship between Morocco and CAF carries institutional consequences for the entire confederation.

In Dakar, Motsepe confronted corruption allegations head-on, suggesting CAF is acutely aware of the reputational damage the dispute threatens. The visit functioned as damage control and institutional reassurance for Senegal. By contrast, his Rabat engagement shifted register entirely. There, the conversation centered on CAF's competition rules and governance structures—territory that feels both more technical and, paradoxically, more existential. The need to revise competition rules points to systemic vulnerabilities in how CAF operates; the emphasis on Morocco's relationship with the confederation signals concern that the country might withdraw cooperation or support during a period when CAF can ill afford institutional fracture.

Morocco's position in this dispute is distinctive. The country has long been a significant player in African football administration and a reliable host for continental competitions. Its footballing infrastructure is modern, its domestic league is competitive, and its national team remains a serious continental force. For CAF to visibly court Morocco while simultaneously managing fallout in other capitals suggests an asymmetry of leverage and concern. Morocco is not merely a stakeholder in the AFCON dispute; it is a stakeholder in CAF's future legitimacy.

The visits also hint at deeper institutional questions within CAF that the AFCON dispute has brought to the surface. Competition rules exist for a reason—to create predictability and fairness. If those rules require revision, the implication is that the current framework has failed to accommodate the realities of organizing continental football in a complex political environment. Motsepe's acknowledgment of this need, even in measured terms, represents a tacit admission that CAF's governance structures require modernization.

Looking ahead, the trajectory of Motsepe's engagement matters enormously. Whether these visits succeed in stabilizing CAF's relationship with both Senegal and Morocco, and whether they produce substantive reforms in how the confederation conducts business, will determine whether this moment becomes a catalyst for institutional renewal or a marker of deeper structural decline. The next critical data point will come when CAF announces any rule changes or policy adjustments—and how Morocco responds to those announcements.
