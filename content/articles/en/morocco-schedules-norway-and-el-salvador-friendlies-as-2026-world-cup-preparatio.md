The Royal Moroccan Football Federation has reached agreements with Norway and El Salvador to stage international friendly matches as part of the national team's preparation cycle for the 2026 FIFA World Cup in the United States. According to sources familiar with the planning, both federations have committed to the fixtures, with formal contracts expected to be finalized before the matches are publicly announced. The move reflects Morocco's methodical approach to building match rhythm and tactical cohesion ahead of the tournament, nearly two years away.

The timing of these friendlies sits within the natural rhythm of FIFA international windows and qualification cycles. Morocco's last World Cup campaign in Qatar saw them reach the semi-finals—an historic achievement that reshaped expectations around the program—before losing to France. That run has created a window of relative stability in the coaching setup and squad composition, allowing the federation to plan with some confidence about continuity. Friendlies against European and Central American opposition at this stage typically serve dual purposes: they provide competitive matches against different tactical profiles while also allowing the coaching staff to evaluate emerging talent and test various systems before the qualifying tournaments later in the cycle.

The choice of opponents carries some strategic weight. Norway, a Scandinavian side with European pedigree, offers the kind of organized, possession-based football that mirrors preparation against certain World Cup opponents. El Salvador, meanwhile, provides contrast—a Central American side with a different tactical philosophy and physical approach. Staggering these matches, likely across different international windows, gives Morocco's coaching staff time to review footage, assess performance data, and implement adjustments between fixtures. The approach is notably professional: rather than chasing marquee friendlies purely for commercial draw, the federation appears focused on building a progression of competitive tests.

For Morocco specifically, these matches come at a juncture where squad rotation and depth-building remain priorities. The core group—players who succeeded in Qatar—will be older by 2026, and the federation must begin identifying which younger talents can either supplement or eventually replace established names. Friendlies of this nature are ideal for blooding new players in lower-pressure settings than competitive qualifiers. The matches also give the coaching staff, whoever that may be by 2026, time to embed tactical identity and build understanding ahead of the qualifying campaign, which will determine Morocco's seeding and group placement.

What matters now is execution. Formal announcement will come once contracts are signed, but the framework is set. Morocco will enter the 2026 cycle with intentional preparation already underway—a marked difference from teams that coast through friendlies without clear objectives. For a program that has legitimized itself on the world stage, this kind of structured planning is exactly what separates genuine World Cup contenders from occasional surprises.
