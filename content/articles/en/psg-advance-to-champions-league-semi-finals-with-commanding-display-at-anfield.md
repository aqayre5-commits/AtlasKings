Paris Saint-Germain dismantled Liverpool at Anfield on Tuesday, winning 2-0 to complete a dominant 4-0 aggregate knockout and secure their place in the Champions League semi-finals. Ousmane Dembélé, the French winger signed from Barcelona last summer, scored both goals—first in the 72nd minute, then again in stoppage time—to settle what had been a tense but ultimately one-sided affair. The victory underscores PSG's credentials as reigning European champions and leaves them facing either Bayern Munich or Real Madrid in the last four, depending on the outcome of that tie.

The aggregate scoreline obscures just how controlled PSG's performance was across both legs. Having won the first match at the Parc des Princes, they arrived at Merseyside with a cushion that allowed them to play with tactical discipline rather than desperation. Liverpool, for all their continental pedigree and their stranglehold on the Premier League title race, found little space against a PSG side now fully settled under their current management structure. The Reds created opportunities—they are Liverpool, after all—but lacked the clinical edge required to trouble PSG's defense or goalkeeper. By the time Dembélé struck in the final quarter, the contest was already decided in psychological terms.

What becomes clear from PSG's progression is the depth of talent at their disposal when operating at full capacity. Dembélé's two-goal haul reflects the caliber of attacking options available to the French club: a player of his experience and technical level can operate as a secondary threat while others command defensive attention. The 25-year-old has had to rebuild his reputation after injury-plagued years in Barcelona, and performances like this—decisive, composed, clinical—suggest that process is well underway. His timing on both finishes showed an awareness that goes beyond mere pace; he read the space and the moment with the kind of acuity that separates functional attackers from those capable of winning European matches.

For Liverpool, this represents a significant setback in what has otherwise been an encouraging season. They remain in contention domestically and will continue in the Europa League, but the Champions League was always their primary European focus. The aggregate defeat to PSG, comprehensive as it was, raises questions about their ability to compete at the absolute peak of the competition. They have the structure and quality to win it, but margins are thin at this level, and PSG's current setup—with financial resources matched to a settled tactical identity—presents a particular kind of challenge.

The semi-final awaiting PSG will be played against a team equally steeped in continental tradition. Bayern and Real Madrid represent the two other institutional powerhouses in European football, and PSG's path to the final runs through one of them. That is the nature of knockout football at this stage: there are no easy routes, only different kinds of difficulty. PSG have shown they can handle Liverpool's intensity and experience. The next test will demand the same ruthlessness.
