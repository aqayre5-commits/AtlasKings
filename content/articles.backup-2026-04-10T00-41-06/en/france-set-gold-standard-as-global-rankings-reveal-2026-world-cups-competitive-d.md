France's dominance in the latest World Cup assessment reflects a squad bursting with attacking talent that even manager Didier Deschamps struggles to fully accommodate. With Kylian Mbappé, Marcus Thuram, Rayan Cherki, and Désiré Doué all competing for places, the French appear to have answered questions about their depth following their runners-up finish in Qatar. Their recent victories over Brazil and Colombia—the latter a 3-1 demolition despite fielding an entirely new starting XI—demonstrate the kind of flexibility and firepower that could prove decisive in a 48-team tournament where consistency matters as much as individual brilliance.

The rankings signal a competitive 2026 World Cup landscape, with Senegal and Japan securing top-10 spots alongside Europe's traditional powerhouses. For Morocco, the assessment offers both perspective and motivation. While the Atlas Lions fell short of World Cup qualification this cycle, their African Cup of Nations success and consistent continental performances ensure they remain among Africa's most respected sides. The expansion to 48 teams means more opportunities for African football's rising forces, with Morocco well-positioned to challenge in future tournaments given their established infrastructure and talent pipeline.
