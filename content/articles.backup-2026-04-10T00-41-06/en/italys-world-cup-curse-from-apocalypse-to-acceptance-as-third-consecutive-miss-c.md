The tone of Italian media's response to Wednesday's playoff elimination by Bosnia and Herzegovina tells the real story of the *Azzurri's* decline. Gone are the apocalyptic declarations that greeted their 2017 Sweden loss or the hellish imagery of the 2022 North Macedonia disaster. Now, the headlines simply read "*Tutti A Casa*" – Everybody Go Home – a resigned acceptance that has replaced shock. This is no longer treated as a shocking anomaly but as the natural state of Italian football, a nation that once owned the World Cup now watching from home for the third tournament running.

The rot runs deeper than recent tactical failures or managerial shortcomings. Roberto Baggio warned of systemic issues as far back as 2011, proposing an overhaul of Italy's talent development pathways. Those warnings were ignored. The national team's current approach – reactive rather than revolutionary – cannot compete with the structural investment and youth development programs of modern football powers. Without addressing how young Italian players are developed from grassroots upward, Gattuso's squad and his successors will continue to struggle against nations that have modernized while Italy clung to its past glories.

For Italian football fans, the despair isn't theatrical anymore—it's exhausted. The crisis is no longer breaking news; it's become the baseline.
